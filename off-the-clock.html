Off the Clock

A little more about the person behind the projects.

If you've made it this far, thank you. These aren't observations about where I went, but about what stayed with me: sports, museums, art, and the buildings that made me stop and look twice.

Most portfolios end with a list of technologies. I thought I'd end with the things that shaped how I think instead. These aren't hobbies as much as recurring themes—systems, design, history, engineering, and the satisfaction of understanding how something works.

Sport

Formula 1 - Watching Systems

I grew up watching Formula 1 with my dad, inheriting his loyalty to Ferrari before I fully understood the sport. What kept me hooked as a data analyst, though, was how much unseen system sits behind ten seconds of overtaking: strategy calls, tyre degradation curves, and pit-wall math happening in real time. It's the exact same instinct for tracking moving parts that ultimately turned into my data visualisation work.

At the Ferrari Museum in Maranello, the thing that struck me wasn't just the cars that won, it was the spaces where the engineering logic was born. Standing in front of Enzo Ferrari's preserved office feels like looking at the original analog command centre of the entire company.

The Formula 1 Exhibition in Melbourne made the opposite point, turning broadcast footage into something tangible. Seeing the Ferrari 312 T2 alongside the densely packaged 2017 Mercedes Hybrid Power Unit made me appreciate just how much engineering lives inside regulations that look simple on television.

Museums

Looking Slowly

NGV Melbourne feels uniquely conversational. My favorite memory there is from the Greek gallery space: an older gentleman struck up a conversation and completely unlocked the room for me, tracing out the underlying layout, explaining how the statues and paintings co-related, and contextualizing the mythology. It transformed a gallery wall from a static display into a deliberate, interconnected system.

The Louvre and Musée Picasso Antibes feel like the opposite end of the spectrum. They are, in a sense, the same institution running two different experiments in scale. The Louvre solves the problem of too much history by making you walk past most of it — you see the Mona Lisa through a crowd and a plate of glass, and the building itself keeps reminding you that art has always lived downstream of power. Yet, tucked away in the sculpture galleries, pieces like Antonio Canova’s Psyche Revived by Cupid’s Kiss manage to completely break through that noise. The interlocking, elegant geometry of the figures draws you in from every single viewing angle, forcing you to slow down.

Art

What was in the frame?

What surprised me most wasn't any single masterpiece, it was how much information sits outside the frame: brushstroke direction under raking light, the different age of varnish across a canvas, and restoration seams you only notice standing up-close. The room around a painting tells you more about attention as a resource than the object itself.

At the NGV, my absolute favorite painting is Édouard Manet’s The House at Rueil (1882). Every time I visit, I find myself standing there just a little bit longer, completely anchored by the sun-dappled facade and the frame-splitting tree trunk. It’s a masterclass in composition and a beautiful reminder that good visual communication is about creating something people genuinely want to spend time with.

Architecture

Geometry made visible

Coming from a computation and mathematics background, buildings read to me as constraint-satisfaction problems with unusually bad tolerances. The Eiffel Tower is a lattice structure doing exactly what a lattice should, most of what you're looking at is air, and the wrought-iron curve exists because Eiffel's engineers calculated wind resistance before it was fashionable to. Pisa is the opposite: a five-degree design failure that's been actively engineered to fail more slowly for eight centuries. I promise the tilt is far more obvious in person than in the photo.

The Colosseum is a 2,000-year-old crowd-flow diagram: its exit tunnels could empty tens of thousands of people in minutes, solving a problem modern stadiums still work at. Sagrada Família fascinated me for a different reason. Long before computer modelling existed, Gaudí solved its structural problems using hanging chains and carefully placed weights. Viewed upside down, those suspended models transformed gravity's tension into compression, revealing the exact geometry needed to support the cathedral.

Standing beneath those branching columns, knowing they began as an analogue physics experiment rather than a computer simulation, was one of the most remarkable engineering stories I've come across. It showed me that elegant solutions don't always require advanced technology, sometimes they require a deeper understanding of the principles underneath.

A final thought

Everything connects back.

None of these experiences are directly related to data science. But they all reward the same habit: slowing down, looking closely, and understanding how a system works. Whether it's a Formula 1 strategy, a museum gallery, or the structure of a cathedral, I'm drawn to things that reveal more the longer you spend with them. That curiosity is what eventually finds its way into my work.
