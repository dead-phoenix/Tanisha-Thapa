Behavioural Economics · Forecasting

Detecting economic stress before the statistics do

How the Behavioural Stress Index turns noisy search behaviour into an early warning signal—and why validating it properly mattered more than chasing higher accuracy.

July 2026 · 8 min read

At a glance

  • Why behavioural search data can precede official economic indicators
  • Building a Behavioural Stress Index from Google Trends
  • The danger of look-ahead bias
  • Why walk-forward validation changed the conclusions

Economic indicators are slow

By the time unemployment statistics are released, households have often been feeling financial pressure for months. People don't suddenly become worried the day unemployment rises—they start searching for terms like Centrelink, mortgage stress, food banks, or financial hardship long before those changes appear in official statistics.

That observation became the starting point for this project.

Rather than replacing traditional economic indicators, the goal was to investigate whether behavioural search data could provide an earlier signal of economic stress.

Why Google Trends?

Google Trends is noisy. Search volumes fluctuate because of media attention, news cycles, seasonal effects, and random public interest. Looking at a single keyword rarely tells you much.

Instead, I combined dozens of financial stress related search terms into a single Behavioural Stress Index (BSI), merging monthly Australian Google Trends data with macroeconomic indicators from the Australian Bureau of Statistics and the Reserve Bank of Australia.

Several approaches were evaluated for constructing the index:

  • Principal Component Analysis (PCA)
  • Correlation-weighted indices
  • Ridge Regression
  • Walk-forward forecasting models

The underlying idea was simple: if multiple financial stress searches begin increasing together, they may reveal economic pressure before traditional indicators fully respond.

The first version looked amazing—and that was the problem

Initially I normalised the entire dataset before constructing the index. PCA was also fitted using the complete dataset.

Although subtle, this introduces look-ahead bias. Future observations influence earlier transformations, making historical performance appear far better than would ever be possible in practice.

The correlations looked excellent. Unfortunately, they weren't realistic.

Rebuilding everything without data leakage

I rebuilt the entire pipeline using a strict walk-forward framework.

For every monthly prediction:

  • only historical observations were available
  • feature scaling was fitted only on past data
  • PCA was re-estimated using only historical observations
  • model coefficients were retrained each step
  • future unemployment remained completely hidden

Performance dropped compared to the original implementation—which is exactly what should happen when leakage is removed.

That became one of the biggest lessons from the project: good validation is often more valuable than impressive metrics.

What actually worked?

Principal Component Analysis

PCA produced a clean and interpretable behavioural index, successfully capturing major stress events such as COVID. However, it struggled as a standalone forecasting model.

Correlation-weighted index

Static correlation weights proved unstable because relationships between search behaviour and unemployment changed across different economic periods.

Ridge Regression

Ridge Regression consistently performed best among the behavioural-only approaches. Regularisation reduced overfitting while allowing multiple search behaviours to contribute simultaneously.

Searches surrounding Centrelink, financial hardship, food banks, buy now pay later, and mortgage stress emerged as some of the strongest behavioural predictors.

The persistence surprise

One result surprised me.

A very simple persistence baseline—predicting next month's unemployment using this month's value—was extremely difficult to beat because unemployment changes gradually over time.

That shifted the focus of the project from predicting unemployment levels toward detecting changes and turning points.

Predicting change instead of levels

Instead of forecasting unemployment directly, the models predicted future changes.

This made behavioural search data much more informative, because search behaviour often changes before unemployment itself does.

Using this framework, the behavioural classifier achieved an out-of-sample ROC AUC of approximately 0.73 for identifying future stress events.

While not perfect, it demonstrated that behavioural search behaviour contains genuine predictive information beyond random chance.

What I learned

This project became much more than building a forecasting model.

It became an exercise in building one correctly.

  • Avoid data leakage.
  • Validate models exactly as they would be used in practice.
  • Always compare against strong baselines.
  • Choose metrics that answer the right question.

Behavioural data is unlikely to replace official economic statistics. Its value lies in providing an earlier warning signal. People search before economies move. Those searches are noisy—but when combined carefully and evaluated honestly, they provide a surprisingly informative picture of emerging economic stress.
